What FixFlow Is

The one-paragraph version, and what to keep in mind while demoing.

FixFlow is a single web app that runs a small repair workshop’s day-to-day operations. It replaces the paper job-slips and WhatsApp back-and-forth with one shared view of every appliance in the shop: who brought it in, what’s wrong, who’s fixing it, what it will cost, whether the customer approved, and whether it has been paid for and collected. The same app also tracks technician attendance, payroll summaries, and a weekly work schedule, plus a built-in fault-code reference so technicians can diagnose common faults on the spot.

Important framing for the demo: this is a strawman prototype. Every screen is real and clickable, but all data is mock data that lives in the browser. There is no login, no server, and no real SMS or payment processing. Anything you change (create a job, log a payment) persists while the tab is open and resets on refresh. The goal is to react to a working shape of the product — “yes, but…”, “we’d also need…”, “this part is wrong for us” — not to use it for real work yet.

The problem today

Job status lives in people’s heads and on paper slips. Nobody can see at a glance what’s ready for pickup, what’s stuck waiting for a part, or who is overloaded.

What FixFlow gives

One board of every job with a clear status, a guided repair workflow from intake to payment, and a live dashboard of what needs attention right now.

Who uses it

The manager runs the shop from a desktop. Technicians work from their phones — built for quick taps with greasy hands and big touch targets.

Two Roles, One App

There is no login. A toggle in the header switches between the two experiences — handy for demoing both on one laptop.

🖥️  Manager

Desktop / tablet · left sidebar navigation

The shop overview. Sees every job, every technician, attendance, the weekly schedule, and shop settings. Creates jobs, sets and approves estimates, marks jobs ready, logs payments, and closes them.

Navigation: fixed left sidebar with 7 sections; wide multi-column layouts and data tables.

📱  Technician

Mobile · bottom tab bar · shown in a phone frame on desktop

The personal work view. Sees only the jobs assigned to that technician, clocks in/out for attendance, looks up fault codes, and checks their week and pay summary.

Navigation: 4 bottom tabs (My Jobs · Clock In · Diagnose · Profile), built with ≥44px tap targets.

Branches off the happy path

Waiting

A job pauses when it’s blocked — waiting for a part from the supplier, or waiting for the customer to approve the estimate. It shows the reason and how many days it has been stuck.

Declined / Abandoned

The customer can decline an estimate, or a job can be abandoned (customer never returns). Either way it is closed with a reason recorded on the timeline.

Home visit

Jobs can start as a home visit instead of carry-in. From the field, a job can be converted — “Haul to Shop” — or the visit can be rescheduled to a new date/time window.

Two status systems work together. The job status (Open → Waiting → Ready → Closed) is what everyone sees on the board. Inside a job, the estimate has its own state — none → estimated → approved / declined — and the payment tracks owed / paid with a method of cash, card, or pending. Every action writes a timestamped line to the job’s timeline.

The End-to-End Demo Script

The one flow to walk live. Take a carry-in repair from creation all the way to closed. Suggested talking points are in green.

  1. Start on the Dashboard, switch to Manager view
    Point out the four KPI cards and the “Needs Attention” alerts — jobs awaiting pickup, estimates awaiting approval, absent technicians.
    This is what the manager sees first thing in the morning — the whole shop in one screen.
  2. Open the Jobs board → click “New Job”
    Fill in a carry-in: customer name & phone, appliance type/brand, the problem, and assign a technician. Save.
    A walk-in just dropped off a microwave — here’s how it enters the system. It gets the next token automatically.
  3. Open the new job → add a diagnosis note
    Use “Add Note” to record what the technician found. It appears in the notes list and on the timeline.
    The technician inspected it and logged the fault — everything is recorded against this one job.
  4. Set the estimate
    Add a part (e.g. turntable motor at Rs 3,500) and labour hours (1 hr × Rs 1,200). The grand total computes live.
    Parts plus labour gives the customer quote — Rs 4,700. The job is now “Estimated”, awaiting approval.
  5. Mark the estimate Approved
    Simulates the customer agreeing to the quote. The action bar now offers “Mark Ready”.
    Customer approved over the phone — now the technician can do the repair.
  6. Mark Ready → watch the SMS toast
    Status flips to Ready and a toast confirms “SMS notification sent to customer”.
    The moment it’s fixed, the customer is automatically texted to come collect it. (In the real build this hits an SMS gateway.)
  7. Log the payment
    Amount defaults to the balance owed; choose Cash or Card. Balance drops to zero.
    Customer collects and pays — recorded here, so the day’s revenue is accurate.
  8. Close the job
    The job moves to the History tab, and the full timeline tells the whole story start to finish.
    Done. And it’s all auditable — who did what, when, for how much.

Design Language

Clean, industrial-utilitarian. White cards on a light slate background, strong type, no gradients or illustrations — colour carries the meaning.

Status colours (used everywhere — board, chips, timeline dots)

Open — newly received, in progress Waiting — blocked on part or approval Ready — repaired, awaiting pickup Closed — paid & collected (history) Danger — declined / abandoned / absent

Typography & layout

  • Plus Jakarta Sans throughout — distinct, not the usual Inter/Arial.
  • Bold, glanceable tokens and status labels; 14–16px body text.
  • Manager = wide multi-column tables & grids. Technician = single-column, thumb-friendly.
  • Responsive: below 768px the manager sidebar collapses into a drawer.

Integration badges

Where the real system would talk to an outside service, the prototype shows a dashed, muted badge with a link icon. These are deliberately non-functional markers, so it’s obvious what is real UI versus a future hookup.

Scope & Caveats

Set expectations up front so feedback lands on the right things.

✓  In this prototype

  • Every screen for both roles, fully clickable
  • The complete carry-in repair workflow, end to end
  • Live KPIs, alerts, and activity feed
  • Attendance, schedule, payroll views & fault-code reference
  • Realistic Karachi pricing in Rs

✗  Deliberately out of scope

  • No login / user accounts / permissions
  • No server or database — data resets on refresh
  • No real SMS, payments, or supplier catalogue
  • No customer-facing portal, GPS/maps, or charts
  • No drag-and-drop scheduling (view only)
Best questions to ask the manager during the demo: Does the job board match how you actually track repairs? Are these the right statuses? What’s missing from a job — warranty, customer history, photos that matter? Is the estimate/approval step how you really quote customers? Which of the four integrations matters most first?
